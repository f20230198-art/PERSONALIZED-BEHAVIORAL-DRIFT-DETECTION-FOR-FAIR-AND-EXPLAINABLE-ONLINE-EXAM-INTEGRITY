% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Online examinations have become the new normal for higher education, but the integrity tools universities rely on are still immature: most are based on intrusive webcam surveillance or rigid rule-based triggers that treat every student the same and disproportionately penalise certain demographic groups. In this paper we propose a framework that, instead of watching students, models their own behavioural signature from low-level clickstream data. Our main proposed detector is a supervised Transformer classifier that operates on action-level behavioural sequences and answers the question ``does this session look like the way this population normally behaves?''. We also retain an LSTM autoencoder as a self-supervised alternative that learns what ``normal'' reconstruction looks like, so the framework supports both labelled and unlabelled deployment scenarios. Flagged sessions are then passed through a fairness-aware threshold-calibration stage operating across four demographic axes (gender, age band, IMD band, disability), and SHAP-based explanations decompose each flag into the specific questions and features that caused it. We evaluate on $\sim$15{,}000 students sampled from the EdNet-KT2 clickstream dataset, augmented with OULAD demographic profiles and GAN-enhanced synthetic anomalies grounded in the literature on cheating behaviour. The proposed Transformer achieves ROC-AUC $0.975$, PR-AUC $0.930$, F1 $0.832$ and precision@50 $= 1.00$, outperforming Isolation Forest, One-Class SVM, a standard feed-forward autoencoder, a rule-based baseline, the LSTM autoencoder, and a supervised BiLSTM classifier. After threshold calibration, all four demographic axes achieve DPR $= 1.00$ and EOD $= 0.00$ on the test split. A Streamlit prototype dashboard presents ranked anomaly scores, exam timelines, and SHAP-based explanations for instructor review.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Remote examinations are, by now, a standard feature of higher education. The shift accelerated during the COVID-19 pandemic~\cite{coghlan2021good}, but it was already underway before 2020 and it shows no signs of reversing. The problem is straightforward: the traditional approach to invigilation does not function when the student is sitting the exam at home rather than in a lecture theatre. Universities have responded by adopting computerised invigilation systems, but the options available are, for the most part, disappointing.

The most widely used commercial proctoring systems---ProctorU, ExamSoft, Respondus LockDown Browser---rely heavily on webcam feeds, screen recording, and audio capture~\cite{nigam2021systematic}. There are well-documented concerns around these tools. They set up an adversarial relationship between student and computer, with an overly surveilled test environment; they raise serious concerns about student privacy; they have been shown to produce higher false-positive rates for students of colour, students with disabilities, and students in non-standard testing conditions~\cite{swauger2020software}. And they are reactive, watching students rather than actually understanding what they are doing.

On the research side, clickstream analytics has been used extensively to study student engagement, predict performance, and identify at-risk learners in learning management systems~\cite{gardner2019evaluating, aljohani2019predicting, brinton2015mooc}. This body of work is valuable, but it is largely focused on patterns at the course level over weeks or months: how often a student logs in, how many posts they read in the forum, and so on. Exam integrity monitoring is a different problem. It requires temporally fine-grained modelling at the question level within a single exam session.

A few studies have applied anomaly detection methods to cheating in MOOCs~\cite{ruiperez2017detection, alexandron2019fraud} and collusion in programming assignments~\cite{li2015detecting}. These methods generally rely on a statistical baseline of the general population---they flag the student who is different from the crowd. The difficulty is that students \textit{are} different from one another. Some are naturally more deliberative, revisit questions more often, or answer out of sequence. Any detector that measures everyone against the same average will inevitably misclassify honest students who simply deviate from the population mean.

This paper proposes a framework built around a different idea: \textbf{personalized behavioral drift detection}. Instead of the question ``does this student look different from other students?'', we ask ``does this session look like a normal behavioural sequence?'' --- and for students with enough history, ``does this session look different from this student themselves?''. The main proposed detector is a supervised Transformer classifier trained end-to-end on behavioural sequences with synthetic anomaly labels; we retain an LSTM autoencoder alongside it as a self-supervised alternative that learns what ``normal'' reconstruction looks like without needing any labels. At the same time, we address two issues that are often relegated to an afterthought in the literature on anomaly detection:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Fairness.} Anomaly detection systems can easily encode demographic biases. If students in certain groups tend to have higher values in the drift metrics because of differences in testing environments, access to devices, and cultural patterns, a naive system will be unfair towards them. We incorporate fairness directly into the detection pipeline through per-group threshold calibration.
    \item \textbf{Explainability.} An opaque score is not useful to an instructor who needs to decide whether to investigate a flag. We provide SHAP-based feature-level explanations for each flagged session, indicating \textit{which} behavioural features (e.g., answer-change frequency, time per question) caused the flag.
\end{enumerate}

Concretely, our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A Transformer-based supervised detector that operates on action-level clickstream sequences and substantially outperforms isolation-based, boundary-based, feed-forward-AE, LSTM-AE, and BiLSTM baselines (ROC-AUC $0.975$, F1 $0.832$).
    \item An LSTM autoencoder formulation of the same detection task, retained as a self-supervised alternative for settings where no anomaly labels are available.
    \item A behavioural feature extractor that produces both aggregated session-level features (for classical baselines) and per-action question-level features (for sequence models).
    \item A GAN-augmented synthetic-anomaly pipeline seeded by literature-grounded rule-based anomalies, used to create a realistic training signal in the absence of labelled cheating data.
    \item A post-hoc fairness-aware threshold calibration mechanism that achieves DPR $\geq 0.80$ and EOD $\leq 0.10$ across gender, age band, IMD band, and disability status.
    \item A SHAP-based explainability component that produces question-level and feature-level attributions for each flag, and a Streamlit prototype dashboard that combines anomaly scores, the exam timeline, and these explanations.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We organise prior work into five threads that, together, motivate the design of our framework.

\subsection{Automated Online Proctoring}
Commercial proctoring platforms have grown alongside online education. Tools like ProctorU and Respondus LockDown Browser combine webcam-based face tracking, eye-gaze analysis, screen locking, and audio monitoring to flag potential misconduct~\cite{nigam2021systematic}. Though effective at deterrence, such platforms have faced intense scrutiny. Swauger~\cite{swauger2020software} documents how algorithmic proctoring disproportionately affects students with darker skin tones, physical disabilities, and atypical testing environments. Coghlan et al.~\cite{coghlan2021good} argue that these tools embody a problematic ``Big Brother'' model of surveillance that erodes trust between students and institutions. Our approach bypasses these modalities altogether, using only anonymised clickstream data.

\subsection{Clickstream and Behavioural Analytics in Education}
Clickstream analysis is one of the oldest techniques in educational data mining. Baker and Yacef~\cite{baker2010data} were early proponents of using interaction logs to detect off-task behaviour in intelligent tutoring systems. More recently, EdNet~\cite{choi2020ednet}---one of the largest publicly available educational interaction datasets---exposes detailed clickstream data from millions of problem-solving sessions, covering answer changes, timestamps, and navigation. Brinton and Chiang~\cite{brinton2015mooc} showed that fine-grained clickstream features could predict engagement and dropout in MOOCs; Aljohani et al.~\cite{aljohani2019predicting} applied similar ideas to virtual learning environments using OULAD~\cite{kuzilek2017oulad}. Hussain et al.~\cite{hussain2018student} clustered engagement metrics from interaction logs. These studies demonstrate the richness of clickstream data but focus on course-level engagement rather than in-exam behavioural anomalies.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection for Academic Integrity}
Several groups have worked on anomaly detection for academic dishonesty. Ruiperez-Valiente et al.~\cite{ruiperez2017detection} applied statistical methods to MOOC assessments, focusing on completion time and answer similarity across students; Alexandron et al.~\cite{alexandron2019fraud} extended this with a more systematic analysis of harvesting and copying; Li and Zhai~\cite{li2015detecting} used clustering to flag collusion in programming assignments. On the methods side, Isolation Forest~\cite{liu2008isolation} and One-Class SVM~\cite{scholkopf2001estimating} remain widely used baselines. Malhotra et al.~\cite{malhotra2016lstm} showed that LSTM encoder-decoder architectures perform strongly for anomaly detection in multivariate time series --- structurally similar to exam clickstreams --- and Chalapathy and Chawla~\cite{chalapathy2019deep} provide a broader survey of deep anomaly-detection methods. More recently, Transformer encoders have become the dominant sequence architecture in almost every domain where they have been tried~\cite{vaswani2017attention}, and we use them here as the main proposed detector. The key novelty of our work relative to the above is (i) the explicit combination of a Transformer-based sequence detector with a self-supervised LSTM-AE alternative, and (ii) drift normalisation that, where sufficient history exists, compares each student against their own baseline rather than the population.

\subsection{Fairness in Educational AI}
Fairness is a first-order concern in automated educational tooling. Baker and Hawn~\cite{baker2022algorithmic} provide a thorough review of algorithmic bias in education, covering predictive models, recommendation systems, and automated assessment; Kizilcec and Lee~\cite{kizilcec2022algorithmic} focus on how algorithmic systems can perpetuate or amplify existing inequities, particularly along racial and socioeconomic lines; Gardner et al.~\cite{gardner2019evaluating} showed that even well-performing predictive models can exhibit significant performance gaps across demographic slices. In anomaly detection specifically, Zhang and Davidson~\cite{zhang2021fair} developed fairness-aware methods for unsupervised settings; Mehrabi et al.~\cite{mehrabi2021survey} survey fairness definitions and mitigation strategies across ML. Holstein et al.~\cite{holstein2019improving} found broad practitioner agreement that fairness needs to be a first-class design concern rather than an afterthought.

\subsection{Explainable AI in Education}
Explainability in educational AI is justified on both practical and ethical grounds --- instructors need to understand \textit{why} a flag was raised before acting on it. Khosravi et al.~\cite{khosravi2022explainable} argue that explainable AI is essential for trust and meaningful human oversight. SHAP~\cite{lundberg2017unified}, grounded in Shapley values from cooperative game theory, has become the most widely used method for feature-level explanations across domains; Swamy et al.~\cite{swamy2022evaluating} evaluated several explainability methods for student-success prediction in MOOCs and found SHAP the most consistent and interpretable. Doshi-Velez and Kim~\cite{doshivelez2017towards} lay out a general framework for defining and evaluating interpretability, which informs our choice to present both question-level and feature-level SHAP explanations. To the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first to integrate a supervised Transformer detector, a self-supervised LSTM-AE alternative, post-hoc fairness calibration, and SHAP-based explainability in the single setting of online-exam integrity.

\section{Proposed Framework}
\label{sec:proposed}

Figure~\ref{fig:system_overview} shows the overall architecture. Raw EdNet clickstream data flows through behavioural feature extraction, then into two parallel sequence detectors --- a supervised Transformer classifier (our main proposed detector) and a self-supervised LSTM autoencoder --- plus a battery of session-level baselines. Detector scores are thresholded and passed through a fairness-aware calibration stage, and flagged sessions are finally decomposed by SHAP to produce instructor-facing explanations.

\subsection{Behavioural Feature Extraction}
For each exam session we extract two parallel views of the same clickstream:

\paragraph{Session-level features (10 features, flat vector).} Used by the classical baselines (Rule-Based, Isolation Forest, One-Class SVM, Standard Autoencoder). These summarise a full session into one vector capturing time, uncertainty, and navigation behaviour: response-time mean / std / IQR / entropy, answer-change rate, keystroke-rhythm variance, pause frequency, question-sequence revisit ratio, speed score, and burst ratio.

\paragraph{Action-level sequence features (10 features $\times$ up to 100 actions).} Used by the sequence models (Transformer, LSTM-AE, BiLSTM). Each action is represented by: time per question, answer-change count, revisit flag, inter-question gap, visit count, relative position in the session, response-time $z$-score within the session, burst indicator, cumulative speed, and gap ratio. All features are $z$-score normalised using training-set statistics computed on \emph{clean} sessions only, to prevent anomaly contamination of the normalisation statistics.

This dual-view design is deliberate: the sequence models exploit temporal ordering, while the session-level vectors give the classical baselines a fair chance on a representation they can handle.

\subsection{Main Proposed Detector: Transformer Classifier}
Our main proposed detector is a supervised Transformer encoder with a classification head. Given an action-level sequence $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times F}$ ($T \le 100$ actions, $F = 10$ features), we project to a $d_{\text{model}}$-dimensional space, add a learned positional encoding, and pass the result through a stack of standard Transformer encoder layers with padding-aware self-attention. A masked mean-pool over valid positions produces a session embedding, which is fed to a two-layer MLP head that emits a single anomaly logit:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{H} &= \text{TransformerEncoder}\bigl(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_{\text{in}} + \mathbf{P},\, \text{pad\_mask}\bigr),\\
\mathbf{z} &= \text{MaskedMean}(\mathbf{H}),\\
\hat{y} &= \sigma\bigl(\text{MLP}(\mathbf{z})\bigr) \in (0,1).
\end{align}
We use $d_{\text{model}} = 128$, $4$ attention heads, $3$ encoder layers, and dropout $0.2$. Training uses Adam with binary cross-entropy on the synthetic anomaly labels, a positive-class weight equal to the negative/positive ratio, gradient clipping ($\lVert g \rVert \le 1$), and early stopping on validation loss (patience $10$). Self-attention is a better inductive bias here than a strictly recurrent model because action-level cheating patterns --- e.g., a burst of fast answers in the second half of a session --- can depend on question positions many steps apart, which attention captures directly.

\subsection{Self-Supervised Alternative: LSTM Autoencoder}
\label{sec:lstm_ae}
A supervised detector depends on labels. When no anomaly labels are available --- arguably the more common deployment scenario --- we provide a self-supervised fallback: a 2-layer LSTM autoencoder (hidden dim $96$, latent dim $32$, dropout $0.2$) trained on \emph{only the normal sessions} of the training split with the masked-MSE reconstruction loss
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}} = \frac{1}{\sum_t \mathbf{m}_t} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbf{m}_t \,\bigl\lVert \mathbf{x}_t - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_t \bigr\rVert_2^2,
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{m}_t$ is the padding mask. Anomalous sessions, unseen during training, produce higher reconstruction error and are converted to a drift score via robust ($z$-score) normalisation; where a student has at least $5$ training sessions, the score is further blended with their personal mean/std to produce a personalised anomaly score. As Section~\ref{sec:results} shows, this model does not match the supervised Transformer numerically, but it is retained in the framework because (i) it illustrates what is achievable without labels, and (ii) the reconstruction signal is a useful diagnostic even in the labelled setting.

\subsection{Classical Baselines}
\label{sec:baselines}
For completeness and to characterise how much the sequence models actually buy, we evaluate against five baselines: a \emph{Rule-Based} detector (thresholds on response time, answer-change rate, and burst ratio), \emph{Isolation Forest}~\cite{liu2008isolation}, \emph{One-Class SVM}~\cite{scholkopf2001estimating} with an RBF kernel, a \emph{Standard Autoencoder} on the flat session-level feature vector (the non-sequential ablation of the LSTM-AE), and a supervised \emph{BiLSTM Classifier} sharing the Transformer's training pipeline but with a stacked bidirectional LSTM encoder in place of self-attention.

\subsection{Fairness-Aware Threshold Calibration}
An uncalibrated threshold can lead to different flag rates across demographic groups even when the underlying model is accurate overall. We compute a per-group threshold
\begin{equation}
\tau_{g_k} = \tau + \alpha \cdot \bigl(\bar{D}_{g_k} - \bar{D}_{\text{global}}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\bar{D}_{g_k}$ is the mean drift score for group $g_k$ and $\alpha \in [0,1]$ is tuned on validation by grid search over $[0, 1]$. The calibrated threshold is kept for a given attribute only if both the demographic-parity ratio and the equalized-odds difference improve; otherwise we fall back to the global threshold (to avoid over-fitting to noisy per-group means). We evaluate across four attributes: gender, age band, IMD band, and disability status.

Note that we also experimented with an \emph{in-processing} fairness approach --- a gradient-reversal demographic discriminator on the LSTM-AE latent --- but found it degraded reconstruction quality enough to erase the detection signal. This is reported for transparency and is disabled by default; post-hoc calibration is the recommended mode.

\subsection{SHAP Explanations}
For each flagged session we compute KernelSHAP~\cite{lundberg2017unified} attributions using $200$ background samples drawn from the training set. SHAP values are aggregated two ways: \textbf{feature-level} (summed over timesteps, to answer ``which behavioural dimension drove this flag?'') and \textbf{question-level} (summed over features, to answer ``which specific questions looked most unusual?''). Both views are surfaced in the instructor dashboard. KernelSHAP is model-agnostic and therefore works identically for the Transformer and the LSTM-AE. Its main cost is compute: the number of coalitions scales with the background size, which is why we cap it at $200$ samples and attribute only for flagged sessions rather than the whole test set --- a design choice we return to in the Discussion.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:experimental}

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{subsec:datasets}

We construct our evaluation corpus from two public sources plus a synthetic-anomaly pipeline.

\subsubsection{EdNet-KT2 (Primary Behavioural Data).}
EdNet~\cite{choi2020ednet} is a large-scale educational interaction dataset collected from the Santa tutoring platform. The KT2 subset includes time-stamped interactions, selected answers, answer-change events, and elapsed times for roughly 297{,}000 students. We sample 15{,}000 students, split their continuous interaction stream into pseudo-exam sessions of 20--50 questions using a 30-minute gap heuristic, and keep only sessions in that range.

\subsubsection{OULAD (Demographics for Fairness).}
The Open University Learning Analytics Dataset~\cite{kuzilek2017oulad} provides student demographics --- gender, age band, IMD band, disability, region --- for 32{,}593 students. Because EdNet carries no demographic attributes, we synthetically assign demographics to EdNet students by drawing from the OULAD marginal distribution, and we pool sparse groups (age band $\rightarrow$ binary, IMD band $\rightarrow$ \{Low, Medium, High, Unknown\}) for reliable per-group estimates. Small controlled per-group perturbations are applied to the behavioural features so that a fairness signal actually exists to calibrate against; this validates the \emph{mechanism}, not any organic demographic correlation in EdNet. We discuss the limitations of synthetic demographic linkage in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsubsection{Synthetic Anomaly Pipeline.}
Neither dataset carries cheating labels, so we construct our own. We use a two-stage pipeline. First, a literature-grounded rule-based generator~\cite{ruiperez2017detection, alexandron2019fraud} injects seven families of anomalies --- rapid responses (1.5--3$\times$ compression on a contiguous block), excessive answer changes, irregular timing (pause-burst-pause), uniform mechanical response patterns, random navigation shuffles, correlated zigzag shifts, and partial-session cheating (a transition point partway through the session). Each anomalous session receives exactly one primary timing anomaly and optionally one supplementary anomaly with probability $0.6$. We use a 15\% contamination rate --- slightly higher than the 10\% commonly used in the anomaly-detection literature, to give threshold optimisation a stronger signal. Second, a conditional GAN trained on the rule-based anomalies generates additional synthetic anomalies at the feature-sequence level, replacing 50\% of the rule-based anomalies in the training split only; sanity filters (per-feature range and NaN checks) reject samples outside the observed feature envelope. Validation and test splits are left purely rule-based, so evaluation numbers never benefit from the GAN fooling itself.

\subsection{Splits, Models, and Training}
Sessions are split \emph{chronologically per student} into train/val/test (70/15/15) using the within-student timestamp ordering. Random splitting would leak future sessions into training; the chronological protocol mirrors real deployment. Autoencoders (LSTM-AE and Standard-AE) are trained on \emph{only normal} sessions from the train split; the supervised models (Transformer, BiLSTM) and the classical baselines are trained on the full train split. Threshold selection for every model is done on the \emph{validation} split by maximising a weighted F1 score, and the selected threshold is then frozen for test evaluation. All experiments were run on a single NVIDIA RTX 4060 GPU. Key hyperparameters are given in Table~\ref{tab:hparams}.

% 6. DISCUSSION

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Why the Transformer beats the LSTM-AE.} The LSTM-AE has to reconstruct the full action-level sequence, which is a much harder target than emitting a scalar logit. With 10 features over up to 100 actions, the reconstruction surface is bumpy; training on clean-only sessions helps but does not fully separate normals from anomalies in score space (visible in Figure~\ref{fig:scores}). The Transformer is directly optimised for the discrimination task and has an attention inductive bias well-suited to the partial-session anomalies (Section~\ref{subsec:datasets}) that have a transition point. This mirrors the broader trend of Transformers overtaking RNNs in sequential tasks once labels are available.

\textbf{Synthetic data limitations.} We are acutely aware that this paper's detection numbers are computed against synthetic anomalies generated by a GAN seeded on rule-based templates. These are literature-grounded but by construction representative of the behaviours the generator knows about. Real cheating behaviour in real exams likely includes modes no generator we built can reproduce, and our numbers should be read as \emph{relative} comparisons between detectors rather than absolute deployment estimates. The same caveat applies to the fairness numbers: the demographic attributes themselves are synthetically assigned from OULAD marginals and do not carry any real correlation to EdNet behaviour; the per-group perturbations we apply exist only to create a calibration target. Validation on a real exam clickstream corpus with real cheating ground truth is the single most important next step.

\textbf{SHAP scalability.} KernelSHAP is model-agnostic but expensive: each explained session requires many forward passes proportional to the background-set size. We cap the background at $200$ samples and explain only flagged sessions --- reasonable in deployment, where an instructor only reviews a shortlist, but clearly a bottleneck if one wanted to explain every session. For a larger-scale deployment, model-specific attribution (e.g., attention rollout for the Transformer, integrated gradients for the LSTM-AE) would be the obvious follow-up; we have left this out here because KernelSHAP lets us treat both detectors identically.

\textbf{In-processing vs.\ post-hoc fairness.} We tried an in-processing approach: a demographic discriminator on the LSTM-AE latent trained with gradient reversal, to remove demographic signal from the representation itself. In our setup it noticeably degraded reconstruction quality, to the point where the detection signal suffered. This is not an argument against in-processing fairness in general --- the constraint we built may have been too aggressive, or it may have interacted poorly with the already-tight bottleneck. It is the reason we default to post-hoc $\alpha$-calibration, which is simpler, does not compromise detection, and was sufficient to hit DPR $\geq 0.80$ / EOD $\leq 0.10$ on every attribute.

\textbf{Cold-start.} Students in their first exam have no history, so personalised blending degenerates to the population score. The framework handles this gracefully (the blend weight $\lambda_s = \min(1, n_{\text{sessions}}/5)$), but the score is inherently less informative for these students and a deployed system should tag the first one or two sessions with a reduced-confidence indicator in the dashboard.

\textbf{Ground truth.} There is no publicly available dataset with verified cheating labels at the clickstream level; we know of no realistic path to one short of IRB-approved controlled studies. Synthetic anomalies offer a controlled experimental setting in exchange for a realism ceiling; we have tried to be honest about where that ceiling lies.

% 7. CONCLUSION
% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We proposed a framework for behavioural drift detection in online exams that combines a supervised Transformer classifier (main proposed detector), a self-supervised LSTM autoencoder (label-free alternative), post-hoc fairness-aware threshold calibration, and SHAP-based explainability. On an EdNet-KT2-derived corpus of $\sim$15{,}000 students augmented with OULAD demographics and a GAN-augmented synthetic anomaly pipeline, the Transformer reaches ROC-AUC $0.975$, F1 $0.832$, and perfect precision over the top-50 ranked sessions, outperforming Isolation Forest, One-Class SVM, a standard autoencoder, a rule-based baseline, the LSTM autoencoder, and a supervised BiLSTM. Fairness calibration reduces residual demographic disparity to zero on every attribute on the test split. SHAP produces feature- and question-level explanations that align with the behavioural signatures of our synthetic anomalies.

The strongest caveat is that our evaluation runs against synthetic anomalies and synthetic demographic attributes. The specific numbers should be read as relative rankings of detectors, not as deployment-ready estimates. A real-exam clickstream corpus with verified integrity outcomes is the single most useful thing that could happen to this line of work next.

% 8. FUTURE SCOPE
% ============================================================
\section{Future Scope}
\label{sec:future}

Several directions extend this work:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Validation on real exam data:} The most important next step --- a corpus of real exam clickstreams with instructor-verified integrity outcomes would let the evaluation numbers actually bind.
    \item \textbf{Scalable attribution:} Replace KernelSHAP with model-specific attribution (attention rollout, integrated gradients) to make per-session explanations feasible at classroom scale.
    \item \textbf{Temporal baseline evolution:} Student behaviour drifts over a semester as they grow more comfortable with the interface. Online or streaming personalisation of the per-student baseline would let the system adapt to this.
    \item \textbf{Multi-modal signals:} Typing rhythm, mouse-movement entropy, and focus-loss events would all strengthen the behavioural fingerprint without adding privacy cost.
    \item \textbf{Federated learning:} Institution-local models with only aggregated gradients shared would allow cross-institution generalisation without moving student data.
    \item \textbf{Causal reasoning:} Not every behavioural shift is cheating. Explicitly modelling alternative causes (anxiety, interface unfamiliarity, connectivity issues) would reduce false positives in deployment.
    \item \textbf{Real-time detection:} Streaming inference during an exam, paired with soft nudges (e.g., honour-code reminders) rather than hard flags, is a promising non-punitive use of the same model.
\end{itemize}
