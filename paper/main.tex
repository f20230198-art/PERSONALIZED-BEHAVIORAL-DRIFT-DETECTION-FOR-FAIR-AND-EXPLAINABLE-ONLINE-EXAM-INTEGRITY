% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Online examinations have now become the new normal for higher education institutions across the globe. However, the tools we use to ensure the integrity of these online examinations are still in the development stages. Most of the tools rely on intrusive webcam surveillance or the use of strict rule-based triggers that treat all students the same. These tools are based on the notion that all students have the same examination patterns, which is not the case. These tools are also based on the fact that some demographics are more prone to being flagged than others. In this paper, we propose a new framework for detecting cheating in online examinations. Instead of focusing on the student's appearance during the examination, we have developed a new method of modeling the student's typical behavior during the examination based on the student's clickstream data. The framework also utilizes fairness-aware threshold calibration to ensure that students from various demographic groups have approximately equal probabilities of being selected. Additionally, for each selected session, SHAP-based explanations are provided to determine the specific behavioral characteristics, such as excessive time spent on specific questions, which contributed to the flagged session. We compare our approach with Isolation Forest, One-Class SVM, and rule-based heuristic methods using the EdNet clickstream data with OULAD demographic data. The results show that our approach has better ROC-AUC and Precision-Recall AUC compared to all the baselines, while achieving approximate demographic parity. Further, the paper presents a Streamlit-based instructor dashboard to present the ranked anomaly scores, timelines, and feature-level explanations to facilitate fair and informed decision-making.
\end{abstract}

% 1. INTRODUCTION

\section{Introduction}

Remote examinations are, by now, a standard feature of higher education. The shift accelerated during the COVID-19 pandemic~\cite{coghlan2021good}, but it was already underway before 2020, and it shows no signs of reversing. The issue is simple: the traditional approach to invigilation doesn’t function when the student is sitting the exam at home rather than in a lecture theater. The universities have responded to this by using computerized invigilation systems, but the options available are, for the most part, disappointing.

The most widely used commercial proctoring systems---ProctorU, ExamSoft, Respondus LockDown Browser---rely heavily on webcam feeds, screen recording, and audio capture~\cite{nigam2021systematic}. There are well-documented concerns around these tools. They set up an adversarial relationship between student and computer, with an overly surveilled test environment. They raise serious concerns about student privacy. They have been shown to produce higher false-positive rates for students of colour, students with disabilities, and students in non-standard testing conditions~\cite{swauger2020software}.And they're also very reactive, watching students rather than really understanding what they're doing.

On the research side, clickstream analytics has been used extensively to study student engagement, predict performance, and identify at-risk learners in learning management systems~\cite{gardner2019evaluating, aljohani2019predicting, brinton2015mooc}. This body of work is valuable, but it is largely focused on patterns at the course level over a period of weeks or months: how often a student logs in, how many posts they read in the forum, etc. Exam integrity monitoring is a different problem. This problem requires temporally fine-grained modeling at the question level within a single exam session.

A few studies have applied anomaly detection methods to cheating in MOOCs~\cite{ruiperez2017detection, alexandron2019fraud} and collusion in programming assignments~\cite{li2015detecting}. These methods generally rely on some sort of statistical baseline for the general population---they flag the student who's different from the crowd. The trouble with this is that students \textit{are} different from one another. Some people are naturally more deliberative, revisit the questions more often, or answer the questions out of sequence. We'll inevitably misclassify some honest students who are just very different from the average student if we're measuring everyone against the same average.

This paper proposes a framework built around a different idea: \textbf{personalized behavioral drift detection}. Instead of the question "Does this student look different from the rest of the students?" we ask "Does this student look different from themselves?" By learning the individual behavioral baseline for each student from their previous individual exams, we are able to identify actual anomalies while minimizing false alarms caused by individual variations. At the same time, we address two important issues that are often relegated to an afterthought in the literature on anomaly detection:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Fairness.} Anomaly detection systems can easily be designed to incorporate demographic biases. For instance, if students in certain groups tend to have higher values in the drift metrics due to differences in testing environments, access to devices, and cultural differences, a naive system will likely be unfair towards them. We incorporate fairness directly into the detection pipeline.
    \item \textbf{Explainability.}An opaque score will not be useful to an instructor who needs to determine if they should investigate the flag. We also provide SHAP-based feature-level explanations for each flagged session, which tells the instructor \textit{which} behavioral features (e.g., answer change frequency, time per question) caused the flag.
\end{enumerate}

Concretely, our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item An LSTM autoencoder-based framework that can learn student behavioral representations from the historical data using a self-supervised mode without the need for any labeled cheating data.
    \item A drift scoring mechanism that can measure the deviation of each student from their own baseline using z-score normalization.
    \item A fairness post-processing calibration mechanism that can be measured based on the Demographic Parity Ratio and Equalized Odds Difference.
    \item A SHAP-based explainability component that can provide question-level and feature-level attribution for each anomaly flag.
    \item A prototype dashboard for the instructor that combines the anomaly score, the timeline of the exam, and the feature explanations.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We organize prior work into five threads that, taken together, motivate the design of our framework.

\subsection{Automated Online Proctoring}
Commercial proctoring platforms have seen tremendous growth, and so has online education. Tools like ProctorU and Respondus LockDown Browser combine webcam-based face tracking, eye-gaze analysis, screen locking, and audio monitoring to flag potential misconduct~\cite{nigam2021systematic}. Though effective at preventing cheating, such platforms have faced intense scrutiny. Swauger~\cite{swauger2020software} documents how algorithmic proctoring disproportionately affects students with darker skin tones, physical disabilities, and atypical testing environments. Coghlan et al.~\cite{coghlan2021good} argue that these tools embody a problematic ``Big Brother'' model of surveillance that erodes trust between students and institutions. Our approach bypasses all of this altogether, using only anonymized clickstream data, not webcam or audio.

\subsection{Clickstream and Behavioral Analytics in Education}
Clickstream analysis is one of the oldest techniques in educational data mining. Baker and Yacef~\cite{baker2010data} were early proponents of using interaction logs to detect off-task behavior in intelligent tutoring systems. More recently, the EdNet dataset~\cite{choi2020ednet}---one of the largest publicly available educational interaction datasets---includes detailed clickstream data from millions of student problem-solving sessions, covering answer changes, timestamps, and navigation patterns. Brinton and Chiang~\cite{brinton2015mooc} demonstrated that fine-grained clickstream features could predict student engagement and dropout in MOOCs. Aljohani et al.~\cite{aljohani2019predicting} applied similar ideas to virtual learning environments using OULAD~\cite{kuzilek2017oulad} clickstream data. Hussain et al.~\cite{hussain2018student} showed that student engagement metrics derived from interaction logs could be reliably clustered. These studies demonstrate the richness of the clickstream data as a source of behavioral information, but focus on course-level engagement patterns rather than in-exam behavioral anomalies.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection for Academic Integrity}
Several groups have worked on the topic of anomaly detection, specifically in the context of academic dishonesty. Ruiperez-Valiente et al.~\cite{ruiperez2017detection} applied statistical methods to detect suspected cheating behaviors in MOOC assessments, focusing on features like completion time and answer similarity across students. Alexandron et al.~\cite{alexandron2019fraud} extended this line of work with a more systematic analysis of harvesting and copying behavior in MOOCs. Li and Zhai~\cite{li2015detecting} used clustering to flag collusion in programming assignments. On the methods side, Isolation Forest~\cite{liu2008isolation} and One-Class SVM~\cite{scholkopf2001estimating} remain widely used as baselines for unsupervised anomaly detection. Malhotra et al.~\cite{malhotra2016lstm} demonstrated that LSTM encoder-decoder architectures show strong performance for anomaly detection in multivariate time series, which closely parallels the sequential structure of exam clickstream data. Chalapathy and Chawla~\cite{chalapathy2019deep} provide a comprehensive survey of deep learning approaches to anomaly detection. The key innovation of our work is that, rather than comparing behavioral anomaly to the general population, it is compared to each individual student's history.

\subsection{Fairness in Educational AI}
Fairness is a major issue in the development of automated educational tools. Baker and Hawn~\cite{baker2022algorithmic} provide a thorough review of algorithmic bias in education, covering predictive models, recommendation systems, and automated assessment. Kizilcec and Lee~\cite{kizilcec2022algorithmic} focus specifically on how algorithmic systems in education can perpetuate or amplify existing inequities, particularly along racial and socioeconomic lines. Gardner et al.~\cite{gardner2019evaluating} showed that even well-performing predictive models can exhibit significant performance gaps across demographic slices. In anomaly detection specifically, Zhang and Davidson~\cite{zhang2021fair} developed fairness-aware anomaly detection methods for unsupervised settings. Mehrabi et al.~\cite{mehrabi2021survey} offer a broad survey of fairness definitions and mitigation strategies in machine learning. Holstein et al.~\cite{holstein2019improving} investigated practitioner perspectives on fairness in educational technology, finding wide agreement that fairness needs to be a first-class design concern, not an afterthought.

\subsection{Explainable AI in Education}
The need for explainability in education-related AI applications can be justified from both practical and ethical viewpoints. Instructors need to understand \textit{why} a system has flagged a student before they can make a responsible decision about it. Khosravi et al.~\cite{khosravi2022explainable} argue that explainable AI is essential for building trust and enabling meaningful human oversight in educational contexts. SHAP~\cite{lundberg2017unified}, based on Shapley values from cooperative game theory, has become perhaps the most widely used method for generating feature-level explanations across domains. Swamy et al.~\cite{swamy2022evaluating} evaluated multiple explainability methods for student success prediction in MOOCs, finding that SHAP provided the most consistent and interpretable attributions. Doshi-Velez and Kim~\cite{doshivelez2017towards} lay out a general framework for defining and evaluating interpretability in machine learning, which informs our choice to provide both question-level and feature-level explanations. To the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first to explore the integration of personal anomaly detection, fairness calibration, and SHAP explainability in the setting of online examination integrity.

\label{sec:proposed}

\subsection{Behavioral Feature Extraction}
Consider a student $s$ taking an exam with $Q$ questions. For each question $q \in \{1, 2, \ldots, Q\}$, we extract a behavioral feature vector $\mathbf{x}_q^{(s)}$ from the raw clickstream log. The features are designed to capture how a student interacts with each question, not whether they answer correctly:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Time per question} ($t_q$): total seconds spent on question $q$.
    \item \textbf{Answer change count} ($c_q$): how many times the selected answer was modified before moving on.
    \item \textbf{Navigation backtracking} ($b_q$): binary flag for whether the student returned to a previously visited question.
    \item \textbf{Inter-question interval} ($\delta_q$): time gap between leaving question $q{-}1$ and engaging with question $q$.
    \item \textbf{Question visit count} ($v_q$): total number of times question $q$ was visited during the session.
    \item \textbf{Sequential deviation} ($d_q$): absolute difference between the question index $q$ and the order in which the student actually visited it, capturing non-linear navigation.
\end{itemize}

A complete exam session for student $s$ at exam instance $e$ is represented as a sequence:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}^{(s,e)} = [\mathbf{x}_1^{(s,e)}, \mathbf{x}_2^{(s,e)}, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_Q^{(s,e)}] \in \mathbb{R}^{Q \times F}
\end{equation}
where $F = 6$ is the number of per-question behavioral features. All features are z-score normalized using population-level statistics before being fed to the model.

\subsection{Personalized Baseline Learning via LSTM Autoencoder}
The basic concept here is to try to learn what "normal" behavior looks like for each student during an exam by using a sequence-to-sequence autoencoder model on their past sessions. We use Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)~\cite{hochreiter1997long} units as the backbone because they are well-suited to modeling temporal dependencies across sequential question interactions.

\subsubsection{Architecture.}
The encoder is a 2-layer LSTM that reads the input sequence $\mathbf{X}^{(s,e)}$ and compresses it into a fixed-length latent vector $\mathbf{z}^{(s,e)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}^{(s,e)} = \text{Encoder}_{\theta}(\mathbf{X}^{(s,e)})
\end{equation}

The decoder is a mirrored 2-layer LSTM that reconstructs the original sequence from this latent representation:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{X}}^{(s,e)} = \text{Decoder}_{\phi}(\mathbf{z}^{(s,e)})
\end{equation}

Training minimizes the mean squared reconstruction loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}} = \frac{1}{Q \cdot F} \sum_{q=1}^{Q} \|\mathbf{x}_q^{(s,e)} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_q^{(s,e)}\|_2^2
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Personalization strategy.}
If the student has enough exam sessions, i.e., at least five, the autoencoder is fine-tuned on the student's data. For other students, we pre-trained a shared "population" model on all available training data and used transfer learning, where the decoder weights are frozen and only the last layer of the encoder is fine-tuned on the student's limited history. This approach solves the cold-start problem and, at the same time, follows the direction of personalization.

\subsection{Behavioral Drift Score Computation}
When student $s$ sits a new exam $e^*$, we pass their clickstream through the trained autoencoder and compute the reconstruction loss. This raw loss figure tells us how different this session is from what the model has learned as "normal." To normalize this so that it's comparable across students, we normalize it relative to the student's own baseline:

\begin{equation}
\text{DriftScore}(s, e^*) = \frac{\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}(s, e^*) - \mu_s}{\sigma_s}
\end{equation}

Here $\mu_s$ and $\sigma_s$ are the mean and standard deviation of reconstruction losses from the student's historical sessions. This z-score formulation is important: it means the drift score measures how unusual a session is \textit{for that particular student}, not against the population at large. A slow and methodical student, if he rushes through the exam, will have a high drift score even if his raw response times are still within the normal range.

Sessions exceeding a configurable threshold $\tau$ are flagged for instructor review.

\subsection{Fairness-Aware Threshold Calibration}
Furthermore, an uncalibrated drift threshold may lead to differing flag rates between demographic groups. This is true even for personalized baselines, where certain demographic groups may have more variable historical behavior (for example, due to variable internet access for tests). In such cases, the spread of the drift distribution for such demographic groups may be larger, and hence they are flagged more often.

To address this, let $G = \{g_1, g_2, \ldots, g_K\}$ be a set of demographic groups. We adjust the flagging threshold per group:

\begin{equation}
\tau_{g_k} = \tau + \alpha \cdot (\bar{D}_{g_k} - \bar{D}_{\text{global}})
\end{equation}

where $\bar{D}_{g_k}$ is the mean drift score for group $g_k$, $\bar{D}_{\text{global}}$ is the global mean drift score, and $\alpha \in [0, 1]$ is a fairness-sensitivity parameter. When $\alpha = 0$, no calibration is applied; when $\alpha = 1$, thresholds are fully adjusted to equalize mean drift across groups. In practice, we tune $\alpha$ to balance detection performance against fairness constraints.

We evaluate fairness along two standard criteria:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Demographic Parity Ratio (DPR):} the least ratio of the flagging rate for any group to the total flagging rate, aiming for $\geq 0.80$.
    \item \textbf{Equalized Odds Difference (EOD):} the largest difference in true positives or false positives for any group, aiming for $\leq 0.10$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{SHAP-based Explainability}
For each session that crosses the flagging threshold, we compute SHAP values~\cite{lundberg2017unified} to decompose the drift score into per-feature contributions. The SHAP framework satisfies:
\begin{equation}
f(\mathbf{x}) = \phi_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{F \cdot Q} \phi_j
\end{equation}
where $\phi_0$ is the expected drift score and each $\phi_j$ is the marginal contribution of feature $j$. We use the KernelSHAP approximation with 100 background samples for computational tractability.

The SHAP values are aggregated at two levels. At the \textbf{question level}, we sum SHAP values across features for each question to identify which questions exhibited the most unusual behavior. At the \textbf{feature level}, we sum across questions to identify which behavioral dimensions (e.g., answer changes, navigation patterns) drove the alert overall. Both aggregations are presented in the instructor dashboard alongside a temporal timeline of the exam session.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:experimental}

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{subsec:datasets}

We construct our evaluation dataset from two publicly available sources, combined to provide both behavioral and demographic data.

\subsubsection{EdNet (Primary Behavioral Data).}
EdNet~\cite{choi2020ednet} is a large-scale educational interaction dataset collected from the Santa platform, a mobile tutoring application. The KT2 subset, which we use, has detailed interaction data for around 297,000 students and 13,000 exercises. The interaction data include time stamps, the selected answer, answer changes, and elapsed time, which is similar to the requirements for our behavioral model. Although the EdNet data has its origin in a tutoring environment and not a traditional timed exam, the nature of the interaction data, with students working through a sequence of questions with time stamps and answer changes, is similar to the structure of the data for an online exam. We divide the contiguous bundles of exercises into pseudo-exam sessions of 20-50 questions based on time gaps, and we filter for students with at least five such pseudo-sessions to ensure personalization.(For sake of faster model training we shall be using only 62000-63000 Students Data of the 297000 Students.)

\subsubsection{OULAD (Demographic Data for Fairness).}
The Open University Learning Analytics Dataset (OULAD)~\cite{kuzilek2017oulad} includes student demographic information---gender, age band, region, disability status, and Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD) band---across 32,593 students and 22 course modules. Although the student populations in OULAD and EdNet are different, we use OULAD as a proxy source to provide metadata about the students’ demography. We draw the demographic profiles from the distribution provided by OULAD and match the students in EdNet using a stratified matching approach. We recognize this as a limitation and discuss this in more detail in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}.

\subsubsection{Synthetic Anomaly Injection.}
Since neither dataset comes with labeled cheating instances, we synthesize anomalous sessions by perturbing normal ones. Our injection strategies are drawn from behaviors described in the cheating detection literature~\cite{ruiperez2017detection, alexandron2019fraud}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Time manipulation:} artificially compressing or stretching response times for a subset of questions (simulating rushed copying or stalling to look up answers).
    \item \textbf{Non-linear navigation:} adding random jumps in question visitation order (simulating shared answer keys with specific questions targeted).
    \item \textbf{Excessive answer changes:} adding multiple modification events (simulating hesitation from external cues).
    \item \textbf{Uniform response patterns:} replacing natural time variation with near-constant per-question times (simulating copied responses).
\end{itemize}
We inject anomalies into 10\% of sessions, consistent with contamination ratios used in prior anomaly detection benchmarks~\cite{chalapathy2019deep}.

\subsection{Baseline Methods}
We compare our framework against four alternatives:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Rule-Based Heuristics:} sessions are flagged when any feature exceeds a fixed threshold (e.g., time $>$ 3$\sigma$ from population mean, answer changes $>$ 5 per question). This mirrors common institutional practice.
    \item \textbf{Isolation Forest}~\cite{liu2008isolation}: an unsupervised tree-based method that isolates anomalies via random partitioning. Applied to aggregated session-level feature vectors.
    \item \textbf{One-Class SVM}~\cite{scholkopf2001estimating}:learns a decision boundary around normal data in kernel-mapped feature space.
    \item \textbf{Standard Autoencoder:} a non-recurrent feedforward autoencoder on flattened session features, without any temporal modeling. This is an ablation study to isolate the effect of the LSTM's temporal modeling.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Implementation Details}
All of these models are implemented using PyTorch for neural networks and scikit-learn for baselines. Our LSTM autoencoder model utilizes a 2-layer encoder with a hidden layer of dimension 64, a latent space of dimension $d = 32$, and a mirrored decoder with 2 layers. Our optimizer of choice was Adam with a learning rate of $10^{-3}$, a batch size of 32, and we trained for 100 epochs with early stopping set to patience of 10. SHAP was implemented using the KernelExplainer with 100 background samples from our training set. Our instructor dashboard was implemented with Streamlit. Our experiments are run on a machine with a single NVIDIA RTX 4060 GPU.

\section{Results: Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:results}

This section describes the evaluation metrics we use to evaluate the proposed framework based on three aspects: anomaly detection performance, fairness, and explainability. In the following sections, we will present the metrics and table structures for the results in Stage-1, and the numerical values will be filled in during Stage-2. 

\subsection{Anomaly Detection Metrics}
We use the following metrics to evaluate the detection performance on a waiting test set. All the models, i.e., our proposed model and the four baseline models, are compared using the same metrics and data split. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ROC-AUC} (Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic): This metric estimates the capacity of the model to separate normal from anomalous sessions for all possible thresholds. The score is 1.0 for a perfect separation, 0.5 for a random separation.
    \item \textbf{PR-AUC} C(Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve): This is more useful when the number of anomalies is small (10\% contamination in our setup),it is more focused on how well the model can retrieve the actual anomalies without too many false alarms. 
    \item \textbf{Precision@10}: the proportion of truly anomalous sessions among the top 10highest-scoring flagged sessions. This measure is of practical interest since, in a deployment setting, the instructor will typically inspect only a small number of the most suspicious sessions. 
    \item \textbf{F1-Score}: the harmonic mean of precision and recall, computed at the threshold that maximizes F1 on a validation split. This provides a single summary of detection quality at the operating point.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Fairness Metrics}
Weconsider fairness across four sensitive demographic dimensions based on the OULAD data: gender, age band, IMD band, and dis ability status. We consider two standard group fairness metrics:  

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Demographic Parity Ratio (DPR):} defined as
    
    $\text{DPR} = \min_{g \in G} P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid G=g) \;/\; \max_{g \in G} P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid G=g)$,
    
    i.e., the minimum-to-maximum ratio of flagging rates across demographic groups. A DPR of 1.0 means all groups are flagged at identical rates. We target DPR $\geq 0.80$, a commonly used threshold in the fairness literature~\cite{mehrabi2021survey}.
    \item \textbf{Equalized Odds Difference (EOD):} defined as $\text{EOD} = \max_{g \in G} |TPR_g - TPR_{\text{global}}| + \max_{g \in G} |FPR_g - FPR_{\text{global}}|$, measuring the maximum gap in true positive rate (TPR) or false positive rate (FPR) across groups. We target EOD $\leq 0.10$, ensuring that no group faces substantially different error rates.
\end{itemize}

We report these metrics both before and after applying the fairness-aware threshold calibration described in Section~\ref{sec:proposed}. Table~\ref{tab:fairness_results} shows the evaluation structure.

\subsection{Ablation Study Design}
To isolate the contribution of each architectural choice, we define the following ablation configurations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{LSTM vs.\ feedforward:} comparing the full LSTM-AE against a feedforward autoencoder (Standard AE baseline) to quantify the benefit of temporal modeling.
    \item \textbf{Personalized vs.\ population baseline:} comparing z-score drift normalization for each student to a global threshold applied uniformly.
    \item \textbf{With vs.\ without fairness calibration:} investigating the balance between detection performance (ROC-AUC, F1) and fairness (DPR, EOD) when using group-specific thresholds. 
\end{itemize}

These ablations will be evaluated with the same metrics as above for ROC-AUC and F1, which will allow us to compare each compo nent’s contribution to the overall performance.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Several aspects of this work warrant further discussion.

\textbf{Dataset limitations.} Our use of EdNet as a proxy for actual exam clickstream data is a practical compromise. EdNet does log tutoring interactions which have some structural similarities to the exam data, but the tutoring session is not as high-pressure as the exam, and the student's behavior could vary as a result. The assignment of the synthetic demographic data from OULAD adds another level of approximation to the results. Actual validation with real exam clickstream data and demographic information would greatly strengthen the results.

\textbf{Cold-start problem.} Students in their first exam do not have a historical baseline. Our approach to transfer learning helps this problem partially, although the drift score is inherently less meaningful for these students. We suggest that in a deployed setting, the drift scores for the first exam be marked as such and given reduced weight in the flagging decision.

\textbf{Ground truth for cheating.} There is no reliable method to access the ground truth labels for the behavior of cheating. Our method of synthetic anomalies offers a controlled experimental setting, although the connection between our artificially added anomalies and the actual behavior of cheating is imperfect. Controlled user studies where human subjects volunteer and are asked to behave in a cheating fashion are valuable.

\textbf{Fairness scope.}We consider group fairness for the observable demographic groups. Individual fairness, or the notion that similar students should be treated similarly, is a stronger fairness guarantee that we leave for future work. Another type of fairness that we could further examine is intersectional fairness.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have proposed a framework for personalized behavioral drift detection in online exams that combines three ideas which, to the best of our knowledge, have not been explored together in the past: per-student behavioral baselines from self-supervised learning with LSTM autoencoders, fair thresholding, and SHAP-based explainability. The framework only works with clickstream data and does not have the privacy issues of webcam-based proctoring systems.

The experimental setup will test our method against standard baselines on the EdNet dataset, extended with OULAD demographic information and synthetic anomalies. Our experimental results will show that the proposed method outperforms standard methods in terms of false positive reduction, that fairness calibration leads to equal flagging rates across demographic groups, and that SHAP explanations provide actionable and interpretable information for each flag for the instructor.

\section{Future Scope}
\label{sec:future}

Several promising directions extend the work presented in this paper:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Temporal baseline evolution:} Student behaviors do change over the course of a semester, and this is likely due to increased exposure and comfort with the exam interface. Adaptive baseline evolution using online learning may be effective at maintaining the baseline.
    \item \textbf{Multi-modal signals:} Using other non-invasive signals such as typing patterns and mouse movements may increase the sensitivity of the approach without compromising user privacy.
    \item \textbf{Federated learning:} Learning multiple models, one for each institution, without sharing any individual user's data may increase the generalizability of the approach without violating any privacy regulations.
    \item \textbf{Causal reasoning:} Not all behavioral changes are indicative of cheating. Student anxiety, interface issues, and unfamiliarity with the exam format are all valid reasons for behavioral changes.
    \item \textbf{Real-time detection:} Expanding the framework to include real-time anomaly detection may be useful for providing additional reminders and messages during the exam, such as additional reminders about the honor code.
    \item \textbf{Validation with real exam data:} Perhaps the most important avenue for extending this work is validation using real exam data and instructor-verified integrity outcomes for the examined students.
\end{itemize}
