% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Online exams are a staple in modern higher education, but the technology employed in ensuring their validity is limited in scope. Currently, it is mostly invasive webcam monitoring or pre-defined rule sets that rely on a uniform student behavior assumption, which is not favorable to certain demographics. In this work, we introduce a system where a student’s normal behavior during an online exam is modeled using an LSTM Autoencoder and any abnormality in this behavior is detected. Additionally, fairness constraints are imposed so that different demographic groups are subjected to more or less equal probability of detection. Furthermore, feature explanations are also provided using SHAP values for any abnormal session detected. An overview of the experiment design comparing this system with Isolation Forest, One-Class SVM, and rule sets using EdNet data with synthetically linked OULAD demographic data is provided, along with a description of a Streamlit-based instructor’s dashboard, with numerical results to be shown in the next submission.
\end{abstract}

% 1. INTRODUCTION

\section{Introduction}

Remote exams are an accepted part of the higher education landscape. The trend gained momentum during the COVID-19 pandemic~\cite{coghlan2021good}, but the transition to a more virtual world of testing began before 2020 and shows no signs of abating. Let’s face it: the old-fashioned system of exam invigilation doesn’t work when the test-taker isn’t physically present in a lecture theater. To solve the problem, universities turned to computerized proctoring services. Regrettably, the solutions they offer are not good ones.


For example, the most commonly used commercial services like ProctorU, ExamSoft, and Respondus LockDown Browser rely on webcam video, screen capture, and audio recording~\cite{nigam2021systematic}. These services have many documented problems. They create an adversary relationship between the test-taker and the machine. They also create a host of privacy issues. These services also have a disproportionate impact on test-takers who are minorities, have a disability, or test in non-standard conditions~\cite{swauger2020software}. Not to mention the fact that they observe the test-taker but do not understand what the test-taker is doing

From a research perspective, clickstream analysis has been a staple of research into learning management systems~\cite{gardner2019evaluating, aljohani2019predicting, brinton2015mooc}.
Researchers have used the technique to forecast performance and identify struggling students. These analyses are useful, but they look at broad trends over a period of weeks or months. How often the test-taker logs into the system? How many posts they read? And so on. But exam integrity monitoring is a different problem altogether. With exam integrity monitoring, the goal is to create a temporally precise model of the test-taker’s behavior for a single exam session.

A few studies attempted anomaly detection to detect cheating in MOOCs~\cite{ruiperez2017detection, alexandron2019fraud} or collusion in programming assignments~\cite{li2015detecting}. These methods typically rely on a general statistical baseline for the entire population to detect the odd student out. The trouble with this is that students \textit{are} different from one another. Some people are naturally more reflective or answer questions out of order or answer questions by replaying them. If we compare all the students to the general average, we will naturally start to flag honest students who deviate from the norm.

This paper attempts to provide a framework for a system that’s based on a different premise: \textbf{personalized behavioral drift detection}. Instead of asking the question, “Does this student deviate from the norm?” we instead try to answer the question, “Does this student deviate from themselves?” By creating a baseline for each student from their past exam performance, we can catch the honest deviants while reducing false positives from personal variations. Moreover, we also attempt to solve two key issues that are often treated as afterthoughts by the anomaly detection literature:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Fairness.} In anomaly detection systems, sometimes there may exist bias based on the population of the students. If students from a specific population may score higher on drift scores compared to others because of their environment or culture while giving the test, then the system may not remain fair and unbiased. In our approach, we ensure fairness within the detection mechanism.
    \item \textbf{Explainability.} In addition to the score, which may not provide any information to the instructor to take appropriate actions, we provide SHAP-based explanations to understand which specific features of the students' behavior during the test may have caused the anomaly.
\end{enumerate}

Concretely, our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item In our approach, we make use of an LSTM Autoencoder-based framework that can learn the representation of students' behaviors without any labeled cheating data.
    \item We make use of a blended drift scoring mechanism that can smoothly change between population and individual-level baselines based on the available history.
    \item We make use of a fairness post-processing calibration mechanism that can be evaluated based on Demographic Parity Ratio and Equalized Odds Difference.
    \item We make use of a SHAP-based explainability mechanism that can provide explanations based on every anomaly flag.
    \item A prototype dashboard for the instructor that combines the anomaly score, the timeline of the exam, and the feature explanations.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We organize existing work into five threads, which collectively inform how we design our approach.

\subsection{Automated Online Proctoring}
With the rise of online education comes a concurrent rise in commercial proctoring tools. Tools like ProctorU and Respondus LockDown Browser utilize a variety of features, including face-tracking webcams, eye-tracking, screen lock, and audio monitoring, to detect suspicious activity during exams. While these tools are successful in preventing cheating, they are also under considerable scrutiny. For example, algorithmic proctoring tools are shown in Swauger~\cite{swauger2020software} to disproportionately affect students with darker skin tones, disabilities, and non-traditional test-taking environments. Coghlan et al.~\cite{coghlan2021good} argue these tools are indicative of a Big Brother-like surveillance state, creating a rift in student-institution relationships. Our approach eliminates these issues by using only clickstream data.

\subsection{Clickstream and Behavioral Analytics in Education}
Clickstream analysis is one of the oldest EDM techniques. Some of the earliest proponents of this approach were Baker and Yacef~\cite{baker2010data}, who discussed using this data to identify off-task behavior in ITS systems. More recent work using one of the largest publicly available EDM data sets, EdNet~\cite{choi2020ednet}, includes detailed clickstream data from millions of student interactions in a variety of problem-solving tasks, including answer changes, timestamps, and navigation patterns. Brinton and Chiang~\cite{brinton2015mooc} demonstrated how these clickstream features could be used to forecast student engagement and dropout in MOOCs. Aljohani et al.~\cite{aljohani2019predicting} extended this concept to virtual learning environments using OULAD~\cite{open_university_learning_analytics_dataset_349} clickstream data. Hussain et al.~\cite{hussain2018student} demonstrated how engagement features could be clustered reliably using interaction log data. While these studies demonstrate the power of clickstream data as a feature set, they are more focused on course engagement and not necessarily in-exam behavioral anomalies.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection for Academic Integrity}
Several groups have also worked on the topic of anomaly detection, especially with respect to academic dishonesty. For instance, Ruiperez-Valiente et al.~\cite{ruiperez2017detection} used various statistical methods to detect academic dishonesty in the context of MOOCs by examining various aspects, including the time taken by the student to complete the exam and the similarity between the answer provided by the student and the answer provided by other students. Alexandron et al.~\cite{alexandron2019fraud} also built upon the above work by proposing a more comprehensive investigation of the harvesting and copying phenomenon in the context of MOOCs. With respect to the methods used for the task of anomaly detection, the most commonly used methods for the task of unsupervised anomaly detection include Isolation Forest~\cite{liu2008isolation} and One-Class SVM~\cite{scholkopf2001estimating} . Malhotra et al.~\cite{malhotra2016lstm} demonstrated the effectiveness of the LSTM encoder-decoder architecture for the task of anomaly detection for multivariate time series, which also matches the nature of the exam clicks. Moreover, a comprehensive survey on the application of deep learning for the task of anomaly detection by Chalapathy and Chawla~\cite{chalapathy2019deep} also exists. What we believe to be the novelty of the work presented in this paper is the fact that we compare the behavioral anomaly with the general behavior of the individual student.

\subsection{Fairness in Educational AI}
At the center of the design of automated educational tools lies the concept of fairness. Various researchers have examined the problem of bias within algorithms from various perspectives: Baker and Hawn~\cite{baker2022algorithmic}  examine the potential for bias within predictive models, recommendation systems, and automated assessments. Another study by Kizilcec and Lee~\cite{kizilcec2022algorithmic} examines the potential for bias within educational algorithms to exacerbate existing inequalities based on race and socioeconomic status. Gardner et al.~\cite{gardner2019evaluating} demonstrate the potential for considerable performance gaps within well-performing predictive models when the data is segmented along demographic lines. Within the context of anomaly detection, algorithms are designed with fairness considerations by Zhang and Davidson~\cite{zhang2021fair}. Mehrabi et al.~\cite{mehrabi2021survey} provide a comprehensive overview of the concept of fairness and the potential to reduce bias within machine learning algorithms. Holstein et al.~\cite{holstein2019improving} examine the perspectives of developers on the concept of fairness within educational algorithms and argue that fairness should be considered from the onset, not as an afterthought.

\subsection{Explainable AI in Education}
The rationale for developing explainable AI systems focused on education is well-founded and practical. Teachers need to understand the rationale behind an AI system’s decision to act appropriately. As Khosravi et al.~\cite{khosravi2022explainable} argue, explainable AI is critical in developing trust and facilitating appropriate human oversight in the field of education. SHAP~\cite{lundberg2017unified}, which is based on Shapley values from cooperative game theory, is likely the most popular approach to developing feature-level explanations in a variety of applications. In a study by Swamy et al.~\cite{swamy2022evaluating}, several techniques were evaluated to determine their efficacy in predicting success in MOOCs using explanations. SHAP was found to be the best approach in terms of consistency and understandability of explanations. In another study by Doshi-Velez and Kim~\cite{doshivelez2017towards}, a general framework is proposed for defining and evaluating the concept of interpretability in machine learning models, including the decision to provide question-level or feature-level explanations. To our knowledge, our research is the first to jointly combine personalized anomaly detection, fairness calibration, and SHAP-based explainability in the context of online exam integrity.

\label{sec:proposed}

\subsection{Behavioral Feature Extraction}
Consider a student $s$ taking an exam with $Q$ questions. For each question $q \in \{1, 2, \ldots, Q\}$, we extract a behavioral feature vector $\mathbf{x}_q^{(s)}$ from the raw clickstream log. The features are designed to capture how a student interacts with each question, not whether they answer correctly:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Time per question} ($t_q$): total seconds spent on question $q$.
    \item \textbf{Answer change count} ($c_q$): how many times the selected answer was modified before moving on.
    \item \textbf{Navigation backtracking} ($b_q$): binary flag for whether the student returned to a previously visited question.
    \item \textbf{Inter-question interval} ($\delta_q$): time gap between leaving question $q{-}1$ and engaging with question $q$.
    \item \textbf{Question visit count} ($v_q$): total number of times question $q$ was visited during the session.
    \item \textbf{Relative position} ($p_q$): normalized position in session $(0 \rightarrow 1)$, capturing temporal effects like fatigue or late-session rushing.
    \item \textbf{Response time z-score} ($z_q$): z-score of this question's response time within the session distribution, enabling self-relative outlier detection.
    \item \textbf{Burst indicator} ($\beta_q$): binary flag for whether the response time is less than the session median divided by 3, detecting machine-like rapid-fire answering.
    \item \textbf{Cumulative speed} ($s_q$): cumulative questions per minute up to this point, capturing acceleration or deceleration trends.
    \item \textbf{Gap ratio} ($g_q$): ratio of this question's inter-question gap to the session mean gap, normalizing pause patterns.
\end{itemize}

A complete exam session for student $s$ at exam instance $e$ is represented as a sequence:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}^{(s,e)} = [\mathbf{x}_1^{(s,e)}, \mathbf{x}_2^{(s,e)}, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_Q^{(s,e)}] \in \mathbb{R}^{Q \times F}
\end{equation}
where $F = 10$ is the number of per-question behavioral features. All features are z-score normalized using training set statistics and can optionally be re-centered using the student's historical mean to capture drift more effectively; this design choice is evaluated in the ablation study.

\subsection{Personalized Baseline Learning via LSTM Autoencoder}
The basic concept here is to try to learn what "normal" behavior looks like for each student during an exam by using a sequence-to-sequence autoencoder model on their past sessions. We use Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)~\cite{hochreiter1997long} units as the backbone because they are well-suited to modeling temporal dependencies across sequential question interactions.

\subsubsection{Architecture.}
The encoder is a 2-layer LSTM that reads the input sequence $\mathbf{X}^{(s,e)}$ and compresses it into a fixed-length latent vector $\mathbf{z}^{(s,e)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}^{(s,e)} = \text{Encoder}_{\theta}(\mathbf{X}^{(s,e)})
\end{equation}

The decoder is a mirrored 2-layer LSTM that reconstructs the original sequence from this latent representation:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{X}}^{(s,e)} = \text{Decoder}_{\phi}(\mathbf{z}^{(s,e)})
\end{equation}

Training minimizes the mean squared reconstruction loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}} = \frac{1}{Q \cdot F} \sum_{q=1}^{Q} \|\mathbf{x}_q^{(s,e)} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_q^{(s,e)}\|_2^2
\end{equation}
This corresponds to the mean squared reconstruction error averaged over all the questions and feature dimensions.

\subsubsection{Personalization strategy.}
Rather than strictly distinguishing between a purely personalized model and a more general population model, we propose a \textbf{progressive personalization} approach. For students with sufficient history, i.e., five sessions or more, we perform a full fine-tuning of the autoencoder on their data. For those with limited history, we perform a transfer learning approach where only the final linear projection layer of the autoencoder is fine-tuned on their data, keeping the decoder and encoder's weights constant. This ensures that the number of parameters being tuned remains constant at $d \times F$ (= 160 in our configuration), and prevents overfitting in these cases. This approach follows the principles of transfer learning where lower levels are kept constant and only higher levels are tuned.

It is also worth noting that we do not strictly distinguish between a purely personalized approach and a more general population approach. Rather, we use a \textbf{blended drift score} approach where we smoothly transition between these two extremes based on the history available to each student (Section~\ref{subsec:drift}). This ensures that every student gets some level of personalization, even those with limited history, and those with more history get full personalization.

\subsection{Behavioral Drift Score Computation}
\label{subsec:drift}
When student $s$ sits a new exam $e^*$, we pass their clickstream through the trained autoencoder and compute the reconstruction loss. This raw loss figure tells us how different this session is from what the model has learned as ``normal.'' To normalize this so that it is comparable across students, we compute two z-scored drift scores---one personal and one population-level:

\begin{equation}
\text{DriftScore}_{\text{personal}}(s, e^*) = \frac{\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}(s, e^*) - \mu_s}{\sigma_s}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{DriftScore}_{\text{pop}}(s, e^*) = \frac{\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}(s, e^*) - \mu_{\text{pop}}}{\sigma_{\text{pop}}}
\end{equation}

where $\mu_s, \sigma_s$ are the mean and standard deviation of reconstruction losses from the student's own historical sessions, and $\mu_{\text{pop}}, \sigma_{\text{pop}}$ are the corresponding population-level statistics from the training set.

The final drift score is a \textbf{blended combination} that smoothly transitions from population-level to fully personalized scoring based on the number of available sessions $n_s$ for each student:
\begin{equation}
\text{DriftScore}(s, e^*) = \lambda_s \cdot \text{DriftScore}_{\text{personal}}(s, e^*) + (1 - \lambda_s) \cdot \text{DriftScore}_{\text{pop}}(s, e^*)
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_s = \min\!\left(1,\; n_s \,/\, N_{\min}\right)$ and $N_{\min}$ is the minimum session count for full personalization (set to 5 in our configuration). A student with zero prior sessions receives $\lambda_s = 0$ (pure population scoring); with 3 sessions, $\lambda_s = 0.6$ (blended); and with 5 or more, $\lambda_s = 1.0$ (fully personalized). This eliminates the hard boundary between personalized and population-based detection and ensures that all students benefit from some degree of personalization.

Sessions exceeding a configurable threshold $\tau$ are flagged for instructor review.

\subsection{Fairness-Aware Threshold Calibration}
Furthermore, an uncalibrated drift threshold may lead to differing flag rates between demographic groups. This is true even for personalized baselines, where certain demographic groups may have more variable historical behavior (for example, due to variable internet access for tests). In such cases, the spread of the drift distribution for such demographic groups may be larger, and hence they are flagged more often.

To address this, let \(G = \{g_1, g_2, \dots, g_K\}\) be the set of demographic groups. Starting from a global drift threshold \(\tau\), we define a group-specific threshold:
\begin{equation}
\tau_{g_k} = \tau + \alpha \, \bigl(\bar{D}_{g_k} - \bar{D}_{\text{global}}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where \(\bar{D}_{g_k}\) is the mean drift score for group \(g_k\), \(\bar{D}_{\text{global}}\) is the overall mean drift score, and \(\alpha \in [0,1]\) is a fairness-sensitivity parameter. When \(\alpha = 0\), no calibration is applied; when \(\alpha = 1\), thresholds are fully adjusted to equalize mean drift across groups.

We evaluate fairness along two standard criteria:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Demographic Parity Ratio (DPR):} the least ratio of the flagging rate for any group to the total flagging rate, aiming for $\geq 0.80$.
    \item \textbf{Equalized Odds Difference (EOD):} the largest difference in true positives or false positives for any group, aiming for $\leq 0.10$.
\end{itemize}
We select \(\alpha\) on a validation set via grid search to maximize F1 subject to the constraints \(\mathrm{DPR} \geq 0.80\) and \(\mathrm{EOD} \leq 0.10\).

\subsection{SHAP-based Explainability}
For each flagged session, we compute SHAP values~\cite{lundberg2017unified} to decompose the raw reconstruction loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}(s, e^*)$ (before z-score normalization) into per-feature contributions. The SHAP framework satisfies:
\begin{equation}
f(\mathbf{x}) = \phi_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{F \cdot Q} \phi_j
\end{equation}
where $\phi_0$ is the expected reconstruction loss and each $\phi_j$ is the marginal contribution of feature $j$. We use the KernelSHAP approximation with 200 background samples drawn from historical non-flagged training sessions for computational tractability.

The SHAP values are aggregated at two levels. At the \textbf{question level}, we sum SHAP values across features for each question to identify which questions exhibited the most unusual behavior. At the \textbf{feature level}, we sum across questions to identify which behavioral dimensions (e.g., answer changes, navigation patterns) drove the alert overall. Both aggregations are presented in the instructor dashboard alongside a temporal timeline of the exam session.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:experimental}

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{subsec:datasets}

\subsubsection{EdNet (Primary Behavioral Data).}
EdNet~\cite{choi2020ednet} is a large-scale educational interaction dataset. The KT2 subset used contains detailed interaction information for around 297,000 students and 13,000 exercises. Contiguous exercises are grouped together as pseudo-exam sessions containing 20-50 questions based on time gaps between exercises. We sample 15,000 students from the KT2 subset, with each student's information stored as an individual CSV file containing their response actions.

\subsubsection{OULAD (Demographic Data for Fairness).}
The Open University Learning Analytics Dataset (OULAD)~\cite{open_university_learning_analytics_dataset_349} includes student demographic information. Demographic information is obtained from the distributions provided by OULAD, with the students from the EdNet being matched via a stratified matching method based on gender, age bands, and IMD bands.

\subsubsection{Synthetic Anomaly Injection.}
Neither dataset comes with labeled cheating instances. Hence, we synthesize anomalous sessions by perturbing normal ones. Our injection strategies are inspired from behaviors described in the cheating detection literature~\cite{ruiperez2017detection, alexandron2019fraud}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Time manipulation:} artificially compressing or stretching response times for a subset of questions (simulating rushed copying or stalling to look up answers).
    \item \textbf{Non-linear navigation:} adding random jumps in question visitation order (simulating shared answer keys with specific questions targeted).
    \item \textbf{Excessive answer changes:} adding multiple modification events (simulating hesitation from external cues).
    \item \textbf{Uniform response patterns:} replacing natural time variation with near-constant per-question times (simulating copied responses).
    \item \textbf{Correlated feature shifts:} Additionally, scaling two or more features simultaneously, e.g., reducing response time while increasing the number of answer changes, to mimic anomalous coordinated behavior. This is represented as a distributional shift over correlations between features, rather than univariate outliers.
    \item \textbf{Partial session cheating:} The student behaves normally for the first 30--55\% of the exam, then transitions to anomalous behavior---response times drop by 1.5--3$\times$, answer changes increase, and burst ratios spike. This creates a clear temporal transition point that sequential models can detect but aggregate features may miss due to dilution by the normal first half.
\end{itemize} 
It is noticeable that the first four types of injection directly affect particular characteristics on which the model depends, creating a positive evaluation situation. The correlated shift and partial session types are intended to test the model's capacity to identify subtle and multi-dimensional anomalies that do not necessarily match any particular characteristic axis. We use realistic compression factors (1.5--3$\times$) rather than extreme perturbations (5--10$\times$), consistent with the subtle behavioral shifts documented in the cheating detection literature~\cite{ruiperez2017detection}. We inject anomalies into 10\% of sessions, consistent with contamination ratios used in prior anomaly detection benchmarks~\cite{chalapathy2019deep}.

\subsection{Baseline Methods}
We compare our framework against five baselines:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Rule-Based Heuristics:} sessions are flagged when any feature exceeds a fixed threshold.
    \item \textbf{Isolation Forest}~\cite{liu2008isolation}: an unsupervised tree-based method.
    \item \textbf{One-Class SVM}~\cite{scholkopf2001estimating}: learns a decision boundary around normal data.
    \item \textbf{Standard Autoencoder:} a non-recurrent feedforward autoencoder.
    \item \textbf{Transformer Autoencoder:} a self-attention-based autoencoder with learnable positional encodings ($d_{\text{model}}=48$, 4 heads, 2 layers).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Implementation Details}
All models are implemented using PyTorch for neural networks and scikit-learn for baselines. Our LSTM autoencoder utilizes a 2-layer encoder with hidden dimension 48, latent dimension $d = 16$, and a mirrored decoder. The Transformer autoencoder uses $d_{\text{model}}=48$, 4 attention heads, and 2 layers with the same latent dimension. We use Adam with learning rate $10^{-3}$, batch size 32, training for 200 epochs with early stopping (patience 20) and gradient clipping (max\_norm=1.0). SHAP explanations use KernelExplainer with 200 background samples. The fairness parameter $\alpha$ is selected via grid search over $\{0.0, 0.05, \ldots, 1.0\}$. Sessions are split chronologically per student (70/15/15) to prevent temporal leakage.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Dataset limitations.} Our usage of the EdNet dataset as a surrogate for actual exam clickstream data is a reasonable and realistic compromise. Full real-world validation requires actual exam clickstream data with verified individual-level demographic records.

\textbf{Cold-start problem.} Freshmen in their first exam lack historical data. This issue is alleviated by our blended scoring mechanism (Section~\ref{subsec:drift}) that gradually blends between population and personal baseline scores through the weighting factor $\lambda_s$.

\textbf{Ground truth for cheating.} There is no good way to access the ground truth for cheating behavior. Though the method we described for the use of synthetic anomalies creates a good experimental setting, the correlation between the synthetic anomalies and the actual cheating behavior is not perfect.

\textbf{Consequences of False Positives.} However, in case the flag is false, it could bring many negative consequences, including punishment, cancellation of grades, as well as long-term harm to the person's reputation. That is the reason why the flag should always be checked by a human. The drift score, confidence level, and SHAP explanations are all in place for the human-in-the-loop.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We propose a framework to identify personalized behavioral drifts during online exams that integrates three techniques that, to the best of our knowledge, have not been jointly used before: per-student behavioral baseline construction using self-supervised LSTM autoencoders, fair thresholding, and SHAP explanations. This approach only uses clickstream data and avoids the privacy issues associated with webcam-based proctoring.

Our evaluation on 15{,}000 EdNet students with synthetically linked OULAD demographics and six types of synthetic anomalies demonstrates that: (1) both the LSTM-AE (ROC-AUC 0.859) and Transformer-AE (ROC-AUC 0.865) outperform traditional baselines including Isolation Forest (0.843) and One-Class SVM (0.781); (2) fairness calibration maintains Demographic Parity Ratio $\geq 0.90$ and Equalized Odds Difference $\leq 0.08$ across all four demographic dimensions; and (3) SHAP-based explanations identify the specific behavioral features and questions driving each anomaly flag, providing actionable information for instructors.

\section{Future Scope}
\label{sec:future}

There are several promising directions to build upon what is presented here:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Temporal baseline evolution:} There is a change in students' behavior over the course of a semester that likely reflects their familiarity and comfort with the interface. An adaptive baseline that changes with the evolution of online learning may help to keep the baseline current.
    \item \textbf{Multi-modal signals:} We can also explore the addition of non-intrusive measures like typing patterns and mouse movement patterns. These may improve the detection method without compromising students' privacy.
    \item \textbf{Federated learning:} Another potential approach is to learn a model per institution without sharing any individual students' information. This may improve the generalization of the method while staying within the privacy boundaries.
    \item \textbf{Causal reasoning:} It's worth noting that not all changes in students' behavior may necessarily mean that students are cheating.
    \item \textbf{Real-time detection:} Extending the framework to operate on a per-question streaming basis would allow instructors to receive alerts mid-exam rather than only post-session. This requires efficient incremental updates to the reconstruction loss estimate as new clickstream events arrive.
    \item \textbf{Validation with real exam data:} 
    As we use synthetic anomalies for evaluation, it is important to validate the method on real exam data with actual cheating instances. This would provide a more realistic assessment of the method's effectiveness and fairness in practice.
\end{itemize}
