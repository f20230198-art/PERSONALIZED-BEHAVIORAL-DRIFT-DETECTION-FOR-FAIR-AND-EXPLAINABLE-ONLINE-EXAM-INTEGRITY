1. WHAT IS THIS PROJECT ABOUT?
================================================================================

This project builds an intelligent system that monitors online exams by
analyzing how students BEHAVE during an exam (their clicks, timing, navigation
patterns) rather than watching them through a camera.

The central question the system answers is:

    "Does this student deviate from THEMSELVES?"

NOT: "Does this student deviate from the class average?"

This is a crucial distinction. Every student has their own rhythm, pace, and
habits when taking an exam. Some people naturally rush. Some people revisit
questions multiple times. Some people change their answers a lot. None of these
behaviors are inherently suspicious -- they're just personal style.

Our system learns what is "normal" for EACH individual student based on their
past exam history. When a new exam session looks very different from that
student's own historical pattern, it gets flagged for instructor review.


================================================================================
2. THE REAL-WORLD PROBLEM
================================================================================

When universities moved exams online (especially during COVID-19), they needed
ways to prevent cheating. The current solutions are deeply flawed:

PROBLEM 1: Commercial Proctoring Tools (ProctorU, Respondus, ExamSoft)
  - Use webcams, eye-tracking, screen recording, audio monitoring
  - Disproportionately flag students with darker skin tones (facial recognition
    bias), students with disabilities, and students in non-standard environments
  - Create an adversarial "Big Brother" surveillance relationship between
    students and their university
  - Massive privacy invasion -- recording students in their homes

PROBLEM 2: Simple Rule-Based Systems
  - Apply the same rigid rules to everyone: "if response time < 5 seconds,
    flag as suspicious"
  - Ignore that different students naturally have different speeds and styles
  - Students who are naturally fast get flagged; actual cheaters who are
    moderately fast get missed
  - No ability to explain WHY a student was flagged

PROBLEM 3: Population-Level Anomaly Detection (Prior Research)
  - Methods like Isolation Forest or One-Class SVM compare each student to
    the CLASS AVERAGE
  - A student who always finishes exams in 15 minutes looks "suspicious"
    compared to the 45-minute class average -- but they've ALWAYS done this
  - These methods don't account for individual behavioral baselines

OUR SOLUTION addresses all three problems:
  - Privacy-preserving: only uses clickstream data (no cameras, no audio)
  - Personalized: compares each student to their own history
  - Fair: explicit mathematical constraints prevent demographic bias
  - Explainable: every flag comes with a human-readable reason


================================================================================
3. OUR APPROACH (THE BIG IDEA)
================================================================================

The system has four main stages, flowing like a pipeline:

  Raw Clickstream Data
       |
       v
  [1] FEATURE EXTRACTION -- Turn raw clicks into meaningful behavioral numbers
       |
       v
  [2] LSTM AUTOENCODER -- Learn what "normal" looks like for each student
       |
       v
  [3] DRIFT SCORING + FAIRNESS -- Score how "different" a new session is,
       |                           with bias correction
       v
  [4] SHAP EXPLAINABILITY -- Explain WHY a session was flagged

Let's break each stage down:

STAGE 1: FEATURE EXTRACTION
----------------------------
From raw clickstream logs (timestamps, answers, navigation), we extract
two levels of features:

  SESSION-LEVEL FEATURES (10 features, for baseline models):
  1.  response_time_mean    -- How fast is the student overall?
  2.  response_time_std     -- How consistent is their pacing?
  3.  answer_change_rate    -- How often do they change answers?
  4.  keystroke_rhythm_var   -- Are their inter-action intervals regular or erratic?
  5.  pause_frequency       -- How often do they pause for 30+ seconds?
  6.  question_seq_deviation -- Revisit ratio: fraction of questions revisited
                               after visiting other questions (NOT sequential
                               index deviation, which assumes ordered IDs)
  7.  response_time_entropy -- Shannon entropy of binned response times
  8.  speed_score           -- Questions answered per minute
  9.  burst_ratio           -- Fraction of inter-action gaps < 2 seconds
  10. response_time_iqr     -- Interquartile range of response times

  QUESTION-LEVEL FEATURES (10 features per question, for LSTM/Transformer):
  1.  time_per_question      -- Seconds spent on this question
  2.  answer_change_count    -- Number of answer changes for this question
  3.  is_revisited           -- 1.0 if question was revisited after others
  4.  inter_question_gap     -- Seconds between previous question end and this start
  5.  visit_count            -- Total actions recorded for this question
  6.  relative_position      -- Normalized position in session (0 -> 1)
  7.  response_time_zscore   -- Z-score of this question's time within session
  8.  is_burst               -- 1.0 if response time < session median / 3
  9.  cumulative_speed       -- Cumulative questions/minute up to this point
  10. gap_ratio              -- This gap / session mean gap

These features capture four independent axes of exam behavior:
  - TIME dimension (features 1-2, 7-10): Speed, consistency, and rhythm
  - UNCERTAINTY dimension (features 3-4): Hesitation and answer changes
  - NAVIGATION dimension (features 5-6): How they move through the exam
  - CONTEXT dimension (question-level only): Position-aware and cumulative signals

Each exam session becomes a matrix of shape (Q x 10), where Q is the number
of questions (between 20 and 50, padded to 50 for consistency).

STAGE 2: LSTM AUTOENCODER (The Core Model)
-------------------------------------------
What is an Autoencoder?
  An autoencoder is a neural network that learns to COMPRESS data into a
  small representation and then RECONSTRUCT it back. Think of it like this:

    Input --> [Compress to small code] --> [Expand back] --> Output

  The key insight: if the autoencoder is trained ONLY on normal exam sessions,
  it learns to compress and reconstruct NORMAL behavior very well. When you
  feed it an ABNORMAL session, it FAILS to reconstruct it properly -- the
  reconstruction error is high. That error IS our anomaly signal.

  This is called "self-supervised learning" because we don't need any labels
  saying "this session was cheating." We only need examples of normal behavior.

Why LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)?
  An exam is a SEQUENCE of actions over time. The ORDER matters.

  Example: Spending extra time on question 12 after revisiting question 5
  is behaviorally different from spending extra time on question 12 as
  your first action.

  A regular (feedforward) neural network would treat the session as a flat
  bag of numbers -- losing all temporal ordering information. An LSTM
  processes the sequence step-by-step, maintaining a "memory" of what came
  before. This lets it capture patterns like:
    - "This student usually speeds up toward the end"
    - "This student typically revisits the last 3 questions"
    - "This student has a consistent rhythm throughout"

Our Architecture:
  Encoder: 2-layer LSTM (hidden_dim=48) --> Linear layer --> latent code (dim=16)
  Decoder: Linear layer --> 2-layer LSTM (hidden_dim=48) --> reconstructed session

  The tight bottleneck of dimension 16 FORCES the network to learn a compressed
  representation of normal behavior. A too-large latent space (e.g. 32) allows
  near-identity reconstruction, destroying the error gap between normal and
  anomalous sessions. The reduced hidden_dim=48 further constrains capacity.

STAGE 3: DRIFT SCORING
-----------------------
When a student takes a new exam, we pass their session through the autoencoder
and measure the reconstruction error (MSE = Mean Squared Error between input
and output). This raw error is then converted into a "drift score" using
z-score normalization:

  Personal drift score = (error - student's_mean_error) / student's_std_error
  Population drift score = (error - everyone's_mean_error) / everyone's_std_error

The final score BLENDS these two based on how much history the student has:

  Final score = lambda * personal_score + (1-lambda) * population_score

  where lambda = min(1, number_of_sessions / 5)

  - Student with 0 prior sessions: lambda=0 (pure population baseline)
  - Student with 3 prior sessions: lambda=0.6 (60% personal, 40% population)
  - Student with 5+ prior sessions: lambda=1.0 (100% personal)

This "progressive personalization" ensures every student gets the best
possible scoring given their available history, with no hard cutoff.

Sessions exceeding a threshold are flagged for instructor review.

STAGE 4: FAIRNESS CALIBRATION + SHAP EXPLAINABILITY
(Detailed in Sections 8 and 9 below)

4. THE DATASETS WE USE
================================================================================

DATASET 1: EdNet-KT2 (Primary Behavioral Data)
------------------------------------------------
Location: EdNet-KT2/KT2/ (61,732 individual student CSV files)
Source: Santa mobile tutoring app (South Korea)
Each file: One student's complete interaction history
Each row: timestamp, question_id, answer_selected, answer_changed, elapsed_time

Why EdNet?
  - It's the ONLY large-scale public dataset with question-level clickstream
    data (timestamps, answer changes, navigation)
  - 61,732 students gives us enough data for personalization
  - Answer change tracking is built into the data

Important limitation (acknowledged in our paper):
  EdNet is a TUTORING app, not a proctored exam. Sessions are self-paced
  and low-stakes. We treat contiguous question bundles as "pseudo-exam
  sessions." Real exam clickstream data with verified cheating labels
  simply does not exist publicly.

We sample 15,000 students and create sessions from their interaction logs.
This larger sample (up from 5K) provides more robust training signal.
The chronological train/val/test split ensures no temporal leakage.


DATASET 2: OULAD (Demographic Data for Fairness Analysis)
-----------------------------------------------------------
Location: archive/ (7 CSV files, primarily studentInfo.csv)
Source: Open University (UK)
Contains: gender, age_band, disability, IMD_band (socioeconomic status), region

Why OULAD?
  EdNet doesn't have demographic information, but we NEED demographics to
  test whether our system is fair across different groups. OULAD is the only
  large-scale educational dataset with rich demographic attributes.

Synthetic linkage:
  Since EdNet students (Korean) and OULAD students (British) are different
  populations, we can't match them individually. Instead, we SYNTHETICALLY
  assign demographics to EdNet students by sampling from OULAD's demographic
  distribution. This preserves the statistical properties we need for
  fairness testing.

  We also inject small controlled perturbations per demographic group
  (e.g., slightly higher response time variance for one age band) to
  create a real statistical signal for the fairness mechanism to detect
  and correct. This validates the MECHANISM, not organic correlations.


DATASET 3: Synthetic Anomalies (Our Ground Truth Labels)
---------------------------------------------------------
Neither dataset has labeled cheating data. We CREATE our own ground truth
by injecting 7 types of synthetic anomalies into 10% of sessions.

Anomaly composition: each anomalous session always gets exactly 1 TIMING
anomaly (from Types 1, 3, 4, 6, 7) plus optionally 1 SUPPLEMENTARY anomaly
(from Types 2, 5) with 60% probability. This creates realistic multi-
dimensional anomalies rather than single-feature perturbations.

  1. RAPID RESPONSES: Compress response times by 1.5-3x on a contiguous
     block (50-80%) of the session. NOT the entire session -- the first
     portion is normal speed, creating a temporal transition.
     (Simulates: student getting help partway through)

  2. EXCESSIVE ANSWER CHANGES: Add 2-5 extra answer changes to 40-70%
     of questions, spaced 2-8 seconds apart.
     (Simulates: receiving external cues during the exam)

  3. IRREGULAR TIMING: Create 2-4 burst clusters of 3-6 consecutive
     fast answers after long pauses (20-60 seconds). Creates a
     pause-burst-pause-burst rhythm.
     (Simulates: alt-tabbing to look up answers)

  4. UNIFORM RESPONSE PATTERN: Replace natural time variation with
     near-constant per-question gaps (3-8 seconds + tiny jitter),
     simulating a student copying pre-known answers at a steady
     mechanical pace. Destroys the natural rhythm that the LSTM
     expects, producing high reconstruction error.
     (Simulates: reading answers off a cheat sheet)

  5. RANDOM NAVIGATION: Shuffle 30-50% of row item_id/answer columns
     while preserving timestamps - simulates revisiting.
     (Simulates: following a shared answer key)

  6. CORRELATED FEATURE SHIFTS: Compress time AND add answer changes
     on alternating segments of 5-8 questions, creating a zigzag
     temporal pattern: normal -> fast -> normal -> fast.
     (Simulates: periodically checking an external source)

  7. PARTIAL SESSION CHEATING: Student starts normally but begins
     cheating at a transition point (30-55% through). After transition:
     response times drop 1.5-3x, answer changes increase, burst ratio
     spikes. This is the most realistic pattern AND gives sequential
     models the biggest advantage -- aggregate features are diluted by
     the normal first half, but the LSTM sees the clear transition.
     (Simulates: mid-exam decision to cheat)

Compression factors use realistic ranges (1.5-3x, not 5-10x) based on
literature review of actual cheating behavioral shifts.

Why 10% contamination? This matches standard benchmarks in the anomaly
detection literature. Too high and the model might learn anomalies as
"normal." Too low and evaluation becomes statistically noisy.


================================================================================
5. HOW WE PREPARE THE DATA (PREPROCESSING PIPELINE)
================================================================================

The preprocessing pipeline runs in this exact order:

STEP 1: SESSION CREATION
  Raw EdNet data is one continuous stream of interactions per student.
  We define "sessions" by grouping consecutive questions with gaps < 30
  minutes. Only sessions with 20-50 questions are kept (too short = not
  enough signal; too long = probably multiple activities).

STEP 2: DEMOGRAPHIC MERGING
  OULAD demographics are stratified-sampled and assigned to EdNet students.
  Group-level behavioral perturbations are applied.

STEP 3: FEATURE EXTRACTION
  10 behavioral features are computed per question per session.
  Output shape: (num_sessions, max_questions=50, 10_features)

STEP 4: Z-SCORE NORMALIZATION
  Each feature is normalized to mean=0, std=1 using ONLY training set
  statistics. This is critical:
  - Without normalization, features on different scales (seconds vs counts)
    would make the MSE loss meaningless
  - Computing stats only on training data prevents "data leakage" (using
    future information to process past data)

STEP 5: SYNTHETIC ANOMALY INJECTION
  10% of sessions are perturbed. Injected AFTER normalization stats are
  computed so the stats represent only clean/normal behavior.

STEP 6: CHRONOLOGICAL TRAIN/VAL/TEST SPLIT (70/15/15)
  Per student, sessions are ordered by time: earliest 70% -> train,
  next 15% -> validation, last 15% -> test.

  Why chronological? Random splitting would let the model "see the future"
  during training, which is cheating (ironic for a cheating detector).
  In real deployment, you train on past exams and evaluate on future ones.


================================================================================
6. THE MODELS (WHAT THEY ARE AND WHY WE CHOSE THEM)
================================================================================

We implement 6 models total: our main LSTM Autoencoder + Transformer
Autoencoder + 4 baselines for comparison.

MODEL 1: LSTM AUTOENCODER (Our Main Model)
  - Architecture: 2-layer LSTM encoder/decoder, 48 hidden units, 16 latent dims
  - Trained on: normal sessions only (self-supervised)
  - Anomaly signal: reconstruction error (MSE)
  - Special power: captures TEMPORAL patterns in exam behavior
  - Personalization: blended drift scoring (personal + population baselines)
  - Gradient clipping (max_norm=1.0) prevents exploding gradients

MODEL 2: TRANSFORMER AUTOENCODER (Alternative Sequence Model)
  - Architecture: self-attention with learnable positional encodings
  - d_model=48, 4 attention heads, 2 encoder/decoder layers, latent_dim=16
  - Same forward interface as LSTM-AE for identical evaluation
  - Tests whether attention mechanisms outperform sequential inductive bias
  - Masking ensures padding doesn't affect attention weights

MODEL 3: STANDARD AUTOENCODER (Ablation Baseline)
  - Same idea as LSTM-AE but uses regular feedforward layers
  - Treats the session as a FLAT vector, ignoring temporal order
  - Purpose: directly measures how much value temporal modeling adds
  - If LSTM-AE/Transformer-AE do better, it proves sequence modeling matters

MODEL 4: ISOLATION FOREST (Strong Baseline)
  - Tree-based method from scikit-learn
  - Isolates anomalies by randomly partitioning data
  - Works on flat feature vectors (no temporal modeling)
  - State-of-the-art for unsupervised anomaly detection

MODEL 5: ONE-CLASS SVM (Boundary Baseline)
  - Learns a "boundary" around normal data in high-dimensional space
  - Uses RBF kernel to map features into a space where normal data clusters
  - Another strong traditional baseline

MODEL 6: RULE-BASED HEURISTICS (Floor Baseline)
  - Simple if-then rules: "if response_time < 5s, flag"
  - Mimics what many universities actually do today
  - Expected to perform WORST -- establishes the floor


================================================================================
7. HOW WE DETECT ANOMALIES (DRIFT SCORING)
================================================================================

After training, the anomaly detection flow for a new exam session works as:

  1. Student takes an exam -> clickstream data is recorded
  2. Extract 10 question-level behavioral features from the session
  3. Normalize features using stored training statistics
  4. Pass through the LSTM/Transformer Autoencoder
  5. Compute masked reconstruction error (MSE, excluding padded positions)
  6. Compute population z-score using robust median/MAD normalization
  7. Compute personal z-score from student's own training errors
  8. Blend: score = lambda_s * personal + (1-lambda_s) * population
     where lambda_s = min(1, n_sessions / 5)
  9. Compare against threshold (tau) -> flag or not flag

The THRESHOLD (tau) is selected on the validation set to maximize a
weighted F-beta score (beta capped at 1.5 to avoid extreme recall bias).
It represents the boundary between "normal variation" and
"suspicious deviation."

8. FAIRNESS: MAKING SURE THE SYSTEM IS NOT BIASED
================================================================================

Why Fairness Matters Here:
  Even with personalized baselines, certain demographic groups might get
  flagged at higher rates than others. This can happen because:
  - Students from certain socioeconomic backgrounds might have more variable
    internet connections, causing more erratic timing patterns
  - Cultural differences might lead to different navigation styles
  - Students with disabilities might interact with exams differently

  If we don't account for this, the system reproduces existing inequalities.

Our Approach: Threshold Calibration
  Instead of one global threshold for everyone, we compute a GROUP-SPECIFIC
  threshold for each demographic group:

    tau_g = tau + alpha * (mean_drift_of_group_g - mean_drift_of_everyone)

  Where alpha is a "fairness sensitivity" parameter (0 to 1):
    - alpha = 0: no calibration (same threshold for everyone)
    - alpha = 1: full calibration (completely equalize mean drift across groups)

  In practice, we tune alpha to balance detection performance with fairness.

Two Fairness Metrics We Target:

  1. DEMOGRAPHIC PARITY RATIO (DPR) >= 0.80
     "All groups should be flagged at roughly the same rate"
     DPR = (lowest group flag rate) / (highest group flag rate)
     Perfect = 1.0; our target >= 0.80

  2. EQUALIZED ODDS DIFFERENCE (EOD) <= 0.10
     "All groups should have roughly the same true positive and false positive rates"
     Ensures the system isn't just equally inaccurate for everyone, but
     equally ACCURATE for everyone.

We evaluate across 4 demographic dimensions: gender, age band, IMD band
(socioeconomic), and disability status.

9. EXPLAINABILITY: TELLING INSTRUCTORS WHY A STUDENT WAS FLAGGED
================================================================================

A flag without an explanation is useless to an instructor. "Student X is
suspicious" tells you nothing. "Student X responded 4x faster than their
average and changed answers 6x more than usual" tells you everything.

We use SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations):

What is SHAP?
  SHAP comes from game theory (Shapley values). It answers: "How much did
  each feature CONTRIBUTE to this particular prediction?"

  For each flagged session, SHAP decomposes the reconstruction error into
  per-feature contributions:

    Total error = baseline + contribution_of_feature_1 + contribution_of_feature_2 + ...

  So you can say: "Response time contributed +0.4 to the anomaly score,
  answer changes contributed +0.3, navigation contributed +0.1"

Two Levels of Explanation:
  1. QUESTION-LEVEL: Which specific questions had the most unusual behavior?
     "Questions 12, 23, and 31 were the most anomalous"

  2. FEATURE-LEVEL: Which behavioral dimensions drove the flag overall?
     "Response time was the dominant factor (60% of anomaly score)"

We use KernelSHAP with 200 background samples from training data, which
is a model-agnostic approximation method suitable for our LSTM model.

10. HOW WE EVALUATE EVERYTHING
================================================================================

ANOMALY DETECTION METRICS:

  ROC-AUC (Area Under ROC Curve)
    - Measures the model's ability to RANK anomalies higher than normal sessions
    - Threshold-independent (tests all possible thresholds)
    - 0.5 = random guessing; 1.0 = perfect separation
    - Our main metric for overall detection quality

  PR-AUC (Area Under Precision-Recall Curve)
    - More informative than ROC-AUC when classes are imbalanced (10% anomalies)
    - Focuses on: "Of the sessions you flagged, how many were actually anomalous?"
    - Important because false positives have real consequences (wrongly accusing
      an honest student)

  F1-Score
    - Harmonic mean of Precision and Recall at the optimal threshold
    - Single number summary of detection performance
    - Balances "catching cheaters" (recall) with "not flagging innocents" (precision)

  Precision@10
    - Of the top 10 highest-scored sessions, how many are truly anomalous?
    - Most operationally relevant: instructors will only review a handful of
      the most suspicious cases

RESULTS (after Stage 2 fixes: question-level features, per-model
thresholds, 6 anomaly types, 1.5-3x compression, blended drift scoring):

  Model               ROC-AUC   PR-AUC   F1      Precision  Recall
  -------------------------------------------------------------------
  Transformer-AE       0.865     0.679   0.620    0.528     0.751
  LSTM-AE (Ours)       0.859     0.626   0.614    0.513     0.766
  Isolation Forest     0.843     0.610   0.582    0.479     0.742
  One-Class SVM        0.781     0.605   0.590    0.577     0.603
  Std. Autoencoder     0.797     0.575   0.513    0.398     0.722
  Rule-Based           0.560     0.230   0.329    0.264     0.435

  Key observations:
  - LSTM-AE now outperforms all traditional baselines (IF, OC-SVM)
  - Transformer-AE slightly outperforms LSTM-AE on ROC-AUC and PR-AUC
  - Rule-Based is worst, validating the project premise
  - Precision is still modest (~0.51), which is expected at 10% contamination
    with realistic 1.5-3x compression factors
  - Blended drift scoring provides personalized baselines for students
    with sufficient history

11. KEY CONCEPTS AND TERMINOLOGY GLOSSARY
================================================================================

AUTOENCODER: Neural network that learns to compress and reconstruct data.
  Trained on normal data only; fails to reconstruct anomalies.

LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory): Type of recurrent neural network that can
  learn long-range dependencies in sequential data. Has "gates" that
  control what information to remember and forget.

RECONSTRUCTION ERROR (MSE): The difference between what the autoencoder
  receives as input and what it produces as output. High error = the input
  looks different from what the model learned as "normal."

DRIFT SCORE: Z-score normalized reconstruction error. Measures how many
  standard deviations a session's error is from the expected mean.

Z-SCORE NORMALIZATION: (value - mean) / standard_deviation. Converts any
  measurement to "how many standard deviations from average."

CLICKSTREAM: The sequence of clicks, timestamps, and interactions a user
  generates while using a digital system.

SELF-SUPERVISED LEARNING: Training without human-provided labels. The model
  creates its own training signal (here: reconstruct the input).

FINE-TUNING: Taking a pre-trained model and continuing training on new,
  specific data. Here: taking the population model and adapting it to
  individual students.

TRANSFER LEARNING: Using knowledge from one task/domain to help with
  another. Here: using the population model as a starting point for
  individual student models.

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations): Method from game theory that
  assigns each feature a contribution score for a specific prediction.

DEMOGRAPHIC PARITY: Fairness criterion requiring that all demographic
  groups have equal probability of being flagged.

EQUALIZED ODDS: Fairness criterion requiring that all demographic groups
  have equal true positive and false positive rates.

CONTAMINATION RATE: The proportion of anomalous samples in the dataset
  (10% in our case).

EPOCH: One complete pass through the entire training dataset.

EARLY STOPPING: Halting training when validation performance stops
  improving, to prevent overfitting.

OVERFITTING: When a model memorizes the training data instead of learning
  general patterns, performing well on training data but poorly on new data.

LATENT SPACE: The compressed internal representation learned by the
  autoencoder. A lower-dimensional encoding of the input data.

PACKED SEQUENCES (PyTorch): Technique to handle variable-length sequences
  efficiently, ensuring padding doesn't affect the model's learning.
